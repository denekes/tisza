Election Analysis / Hungary 2026

The Supermajority Paradox: Hungary's Election and the Fidesz Restoration

Why winning the election without 133 seats may guarantee that Orban's system survives, and what that means for 2030
Daniel Enekes  /  April 5, 2026  /  Election Day: April 12

Hungary's 199-seat National Assembly requires 133 seats for a two-thirds constitutional supermajority. Every independent pollster shows Tisza ahead. But "ahead" is not "dominant." And winning without a supermajority may be worse than it sounds. This paper makes a two-part argument: first, that Tisza's chances of reaching 133 seats are roughly 12-16%. Second, and more importantly, that falling short of that threshold creates a 60-70% probability of Fidesz returning to power by 2030. The supermajority question is not an academic footnote. It is the entire ballgame.

02The Electoral System: Votes to Seats

Hungary uses a mixed-member majoritarian system that was specifically redesigned by Fidesz after 2010 to amplify its structural advantages. The 199 seats split into 106 single-member districts (winner-take-all) and 93 national list seats (modified proportional, d'Hondt method).

Three features create a "conversion gap" between popular vote and seat share:

Gerrymandering

Fidesz redrew all 106 constituency boundaries after 2010. Research by Political Capital found that with equal vote shares, Fidesz would still win 58 districts vs. 48 for the opposition. In late 2024, Budapest was reduced from 18 to 16 seats while Pest County expanded from 12 to 14. Independent analysts estimate Tisza needs a 3 to 5 point popular vote lead just to break even on seats.

Winner Compensation

Surplus votes (margin of victory minus one) recycle to the winning party's national list. This means the system rewards the party winning more districts by narrow margins. In 2022, Fidesz converted 53% of the popular vote into 68% of seats (135/199).

Diaspora Votes

A record 497,000 Hungarians abroad registered for 2026. Historically 80-86% vote Fidesz. At projected turnout, this adds ~250,000 votes to the Fidesz national list, roughly 2 extra seats.

04What Has to Be True: Six Conditions

For Tisza to reach 133 seats, all of the following must hold simultaneously:

Condition 1: Popular Vote Margin of 15+ Points

35-45%

Tisza needs 52-55% vs. 37-40% among all voters, not just decided. With 20-26% undecided, there is enormous room for compression. Undecided voters have historically broken slightly toward the incumbent.

Condition 2: Win 70+ of 106 Districts

20-30%

In 2022, the united opposition won only 18 of 106 districts (17 in Budapest, 1 elsewhere). Tisza would need 50+ wins outside Budapest. Its "Tisza Islands" ground network is new and untested against Fidesz's 16-year rural entrenchment.

Condition 3: Mi Hazank Stays Above 5%

~60%

The far-right party polls at 5-6%, right at the parliamentary threshold. If it enters parliament, it splits the right-wing vote. If it falls below 5%, its votes are wasted and Fidesz benefits in list allocation.

Condition 4: Turnout Exceeds 72%

~50%

Hungary has seen 61-70% turnout since 2014. Higher turnout brings out irregular voters who skew anti-Fidesz. 72%+ would be a post-1990 record.

Condition 5: No Systematic Poll Error Favoring Tisza

~50%

In 2022, independent polls showed a competitive race. Fidesz outperformed by 5-8 points. If a similar systematic error exists in 2026, the actual margin could be 10+ points smaller than surveys suggest.

Condition 6: Free and Fair Vote Count

~85%

International observers rate Hungary's elections as "free but not fair." The structural playing field is tilted, but physical ballot manipulation is unlikely.

Model transparency: The single most consequential assumption is the 4% structural bias (gerrymandering penalty). Political Capital's research supports this estimate, but the 2024 boundary changes may have increased it. The reader should judge whether 4% is aggressive or conservative.

07How Much Do I Agree?

My estimate of 12-16% sits meaningfully below Polymarket's 26%. Three reasons:

The conversion math is brutal. Even at +19 nationally, the district-level arithmetic in gerrymandered rural constituencies makes winning 70+ of 106 districts extremely difficult for a party without established local infrastructure.

Undecided voters create downside risk. With 20-26% undecided one week out, there is significant room for margin compression. Hungarian undecideds have historically broken slightly toward the incumbent.

The 2022 precedent haunts. Polls showed a competitive race. Fidesz won by 20 points. The independent polling community has not fully explained what went wrong.

But even at 12-16%, the supermajority question demands more analysis than a probability estimate. The real question is: what happens in the 84-88% of scenarios where Tisza falls short? The answer, as the next two sections argue, is that Fidesz's constitutional architecture activates as designed, trapping the new government in a cage it cannot escape, and creating a 60-70% probability of Fidesz restoration by 2030.

Most Likely Outcome

Tisza wins 100-125 seats. A strong majority. Magyar becomes PM. But no constitutional supermajority.

This means Orban's constitutional architecture stays intact. The Fidesz-era Basic Law, cardinal laws, and packed institutions all survive. A Tisza government governs within Orban's framework, unable to change the rules of the game.

The gap between 129 and 133 is not a rounding error. It is the difference between continuity and transformation.

But the consequences extend further. The next two sections demonstrate that a simple majority without 2/3 creates a 60-70% probability that Fidesz returns to power by 2030, completing the restoration cycle.

08If Not 133: The Fidesz Restoration Scenario (2030)

The most likely outcome of April 12 is a Tisza majority without a supermajority. This raises a question with enormous consequences: will Tisza hold together long enough to govern effectively, or will the structural traps Fidesz has built cause it to fragment, opening the door to a Fidesz restoration by 2030?

Orban's Constitutional Prison

Fidesz spent 16 years building a legal architecture specifically designed to survive an electoral loss. A Tisza government without 133 seats inherits:

Cardinal Laws (2/3 Required to Change)

Locked

The judiciary, media regulation, electoral system, public finances, family policy, church relations, pension system, and taxation are all governed by "cardinal laws" that require a two-thirds supermajority to amend. A Tisza government with 120 seats cannot touch any of them.

Packed Constitutional Court

15/15 Fidesz

All 15 Constitutional Court judges were appointed by Fidesz. They can strike down any legislation that conflicts with the Fidesz-era Basic Law. Not enough judges rotate off during a single term to break the lock.

Fidesz-Loyal President (Until 2029)

Veto Power

President Tamas Sulyok, close to Fidesz, remains in office until 2029. He can refer laws to the Constitutional Court, refuse judicial appointments, and, critically, call early elections if the government fails to pass a budget. Removing the president requires a two-thirds vote.

National Budget Council

Veto Power

The Budget Council can veto any budget that adds to national debt. Its members were appointed by Fidesz for 6-12 year terms. If they veto the budget, the president can dissolve parliament and call new elections. This is the "kill switch" for any Tisza government.

Chief Prosecutor (Until 2034)

Locked

The Fidesz-appointed chief prosecutor serves until 2034. Anti-corruption prosecutions against Fidesz figures require his cooperation. Without a 2/3 majority, Tisza cannot replace him.

The trap in plain language: Tisza can win the election but cannot change the rules of the game. Every significant reform hits a Fidesz veto point. The cardinal laws, the court, the president, and the Budget Council create an institutional cage designed to make a non-Fidesz government fail.

Will Tisza Hold Together?

Tisza faces a fragmentation risk that Fidesz never had. Four structural factors make the party vulnerable:

Factor 1: One-Man Party

High Risk

Tisza is, by virtually all analyses, synonymous with Peter Magyar. Polish political analyst Maciej Szymanowski calls it "a one-man show." Magyar had never led a team before becoming party president in 2024 at age 43. The party has no second-tier leadership with independent public profiles. If Magyar stumbles, there is no succession plan.

Factor 2: Ideologically Incoherent Base

Medium Risk

Tisza's voter base is a coalition of convenience: former Fidesz supporters, former left-liberal voters, and first-time voters, all united by opposition to Orban rather than a shared positive vision. ISPI notes Magyar "avoids taking clear positions on sensitive issues including migration, LGBTQ+ rights, university autonomy and the role of NGOs." Once Orban is gone, the glue dissolves.

Factor 3: Zero Governing Experience

High Risk

Tisza has never governed anything. The party contested its first election (EP 2024) barely two years ago. Its 106 parliamentary candidates were selected through primaries in late 2025. Most have never held public office. The jump from campaigning to managing a state apparatus designed to resist you is enormous.

Factor 4: The EU Funds Deadline Trap

Medium Risk

Tisza has promised to unlock ~18 billion euros in frozen EU funds. But Brussels requires specific rule-of-law reforms by August 2026, reforms that may require changing cardinal laws Tisza cannot change without 2/3. If the funds stay frozen, Tisza's core promise fails within months of taking office.

Fidesz as an Opposition Force

Fidesz has extensive experience as an opposition party, and a track record of weaponizing crisis from opposition. The 2006 precedent is instructive: Fidesz lost the election, but within months, the leaked "Oszod speech" by Socialist PM Gyurcsany triggered street protests that Fidesz amplified into a permanent delegitimization campaign. By 2010, Fidesz won a supermajority.

09Recursive Probability: Fidesz Restoration by 2030

The question reduces to a chain of conditional probabilities. For Fidesz to return to power by 2030, a sequence of conditions must hold:

Condition A: Tisza Wins Without Supermajority

~80%

If Tisza wins at all (68-70% likely per Polymarket), the conditional probability of supermajority given winning is roughly 18-23% (our 12-16% unconditional estimate divided by the 68% win probability). So in roughly 4 out of 5 Tisza-wins scenarios, they win without 2/3. This is the dominant scenario.

Condition B: Institutional Traps Activate

~75%

The Constitutional Court blocks key legislation. The Budget Council threatens budget vetoes. The president refers laws for review. The cardinal laws prevent electoral and judicial reform. Given the design of these traps, activation is near-certain. The only escape is invoking EU law supremacy (the "Scheppele doctrine"), which is legally untested and politically explosive.

Condition C: Tisza Fails to Deliver on Core Promises Within 2 Years

~55%

EU funds stay partly frozen. Corruption prosecutions are blocked by the chief prosecutor. Healthcare and education reform require cardinal law changes. Economic performance disappoints. The "Hungarian New Deal" stalls. Voter disillusionment sets in by 2028.

Condition D: Tisza Fragments or Loses Internal Cohesion

~40%

The ideologically diverse coalition begins splitting as governing forces prioritization. Former left voters clash with former Fidesz voters on social policy. Magyar's leadership style faces internal resistance. Individual MPs defect or form splinter groups. Without a shared enemy (Orban in power), the centrifugal forces accelerate.

Condition E: Fidesz Maintains Unity and Avoids Leadership Crisis

~60%

Orban (age 67 in 2030) remains party leader or orchestrates a smooth succession. No major corruption trial destabilizes the party. The 2006-2010 opposition playbook is replicated: permanent delegitimization campaign, institutional obstruction, wait for the government to fail. Counter-scenario: electoral loss triggers internal reckoning, Orban retires, party fractures between establishment and far-right wings.

Condition F: Electoral System Remains Unchanged

~85%

Without 2/3, Tisza cannot change the gerrymandered districts, the winner compensation system, or the diaspora voting rules. The same structural advantages that gave Fidesz 68% of seats with 53% of votes in 2022 will still be in place in 2030.

The relevant reference class is: democratic transitions where a new government won power from an entrenched incumbent but lacked the constitutional authority to dismantle the old regime's institutional architecture.

What the Reference Class Shows

Of 9 comparable cases, 7 resulted in the old regime (or its direct successor) returning to power within one or two election cycles. The two exceptions (Ecuador, Georgia) involved fragmentation or a different form of regime change rather than straightforward restoration.

Three patterns emerge across the cases:

Pattern 1: The "glue dissolves" problem. In Israel (2021), Slovakia (2020), and Argentina (2015), the new government was a coalition of convenience united by opposition to the incumbent rather than shared ideology. Once in power, internal contradictions tore it apart. Tisza's base of former Fidesz voters, former left-liberals, and first-time voters mirrors this pattern exactly.

Pattern 2: The institutional cage. In Poland (2007-2015) and Hungary (2002-2010), the new government could not dismantle the predecessor's institutional advantages. The predecessor used its remaining institutional power to obstruct, delegitimize, and wait. The new government bled legitimacy through inability to deliver reform. Hungary's 1990-1994 MDF government is the earliest version of this pattern: the first democratic government inherited a state apparatus still staffed by the old regime's people and could not transform it fast enough.

Pattern 3: The economic trap. In Argentina (2015-2019), the new government inherited structural economic problems it could not solve within one term. Voter patience expired before results materialized. Hungary's stagnant economy, frozen EU funds, and cardinal-law constraints on fiscal policy create an analogous trap.

The key difference from the reference class: Poland 2023 is the closest analogue to Hungary 2026. Tusk faces a hostile president (until mid-2025), a packed Constitutional Tribunal, and PiS-era laws he cannot change without supermajority. Tusk has navigated around some traps by invoking EU law and simply ignoring the captured Tribunal's rulings. If Magyar follows Tusk's playbook, the restoration probability drops. But Tusk has 30 years of governing experience and a deep party apparatus. Magyar has neither.

The Historical Pattern

Hungary's own history reinforces the reference class. Every change government since 1990 has failed to consolidate: the MDF's democratic transition government (1990-1994) was replaced by the post-communist socialists; the MSZP under Horn (1994-1998) lasted one term before Fidesz won; the MSZP's return under Medgyessy/Gyurcsany (2002-2010) collapsed after the Oszod speech crisis. Only Fidesz has held power for more than one full cycle, by redesigning the system to prevent alternation. The structural logic is straightforward: Fidesz built a system that makes governing without a supermajority nearly impossible. Any non-Fidesz government operates inside a constitutional cage, unable to deliver on promises, bleeding legitimacy, and facing institutional obstruction at every turn.

The most likely four-year trajectory follows the pattern visible across the reference class: initial public enthusiasm, followed by institutional resistance, followed by delivery failure, followed by voter disillusionment, followed by restoration of the old regime.

The Restoration Thesis

If Tisza wins without 2/3, the most likely four-year trajectory is: initial enthusiasm, institutional resistance, delivery failure, voter disillusionment, Fidesz return. The probability of this cycle completing by 2030 is 60-70%.

The only circuit-breaker is if Tisza finds a legal mechanism to override the cardinal laws, most likely by invoking EU law supremacy (Article 2 TEU) to set aside domestic supermajority requirements that violate democratic norms. This is the "Scheppele doctrine," proposed by Princeton's Kim Lane Scheppele. It is legally plausible but politically explosive, and no government has ever attempted it.

Without the supermajority, Tisza's best realistic path is to govern competently enough to build a case for 133 seats in 2030. But the same institutional traps that prevent reform also prevent the visible results needed to justify re-election. This is the paradox at the heart of Hungary's democratic transition: the system is designed so that only the party that built the system can operate it effectively.

The 2026 election may decide who governs Hungary for four years. Only the supermajority question decides whether Hungary's democratic architecture can actually change.

A kétharmados paradoxon: a magyar választás és a Fidesz-restauráció

Miért garantálhatja Orbán rendszerének túlélését, ha a Tisza 133 mandátum nélkül nyer, és mit jelent ez 2030-ra nézve
Enekes Dániel  /  2026. április 5.  /  Választás napja: április 12.

A 199 fős Országgyűlésben 133 mandátum szükséges a kétharmados alkotmányos többséghez. Minden független közvélemény-kutató intézet a Tisza előnyét mutatja. De az „előny" nem jelent „dominanciát." A kétharmad nélküli győzelem pedig rosszabb lehet, mint amilyennek hangzik. Ez az elemzés két állítást tesz. Egyrészt: a Tisza esélye a 133 mandátum elérésére mindössze 12-16%-ra tehető. Másrészt, és ami fontosabb: ha nem éri el ezt a küszöböt, 60-70%-os valószínűséggel tér vissza a Fidesz 2030-ra. A kétharmad kérdése nem lábjegyzet. Ez az egész játék tétje.

A Fidesz 2010 után újrarajzolta mind a 106 választókerület határát. A Political Capital kutatása szerint egyenlő szavazati arány esetén a Fidesz még mindig 58 kerületet nyerne a 48-cal szemben. 2024 végén Budapest választókerületeinek száma 18-ról 16-ra csökkent, míg Pest megyéé 12-ről 14-re nőtt. Független elemzők szerint a Tiszának 3-5 pontos szavazati előnyre van szüksége pusztán a mandátumparitáshoz.

Győzteskompenzáció

A többletszavazatok (a győzelmi többlet mínusz egy) a győztes párt országos listájára kerülnek. 2022-ben a Fidesz a szavazatok 53%-át a mandátumok 68%-ára váltotta (135/199).

Diaszpóra-szavazatok

~50%

~85%

07Az én értékelésem

A 12-16%-os becslésem érdemben alacsonyabb a Polymarket 26%-ánál. Három ok:

A konverziós matematika kegyetlen. Még +19 pontos országos előnnyel is rendkívül megnehezíti 70+ kerület megnyerését a gerrymanderelt vidéki kerületek aritmetikája, különösen egy helyi infrastruktúra nélküli párt számára.

A bizonytalan szavazók lefelé mutató kockázatot jelentenek. 20-26% bizonytalan szavazó egy héttel a választás előtt. A magyar bizonytalan szavazók történelmileg enyhén a kormányzó pártot részesítették előnyben.

A 2022-es precedens kísérti az elemzést. A közvélemény-kutatások szoros versenyt mutattak. A Fidesz 20 ponttal nyert.

De még 12-16%-nál is a kétharmad kérdése több elemzést igényel. Az igazi kérdés: mi történik a 84-88%-nyi forgatókönyvben, ahol a Tisza nem éri el a kétharmadot? A válasz egyértelmű. A Fidesz alkotmányos architektúrája a tervek szerint aktiválódik. Az új kormányt bezárja egy ketrecbe, amelyből nem tud kiszabadulni. A Fidesz 2030-as restaurációjának valószínűsége ebben az esetben 60-70%.

Legvalószínűbb kimenetel

A Tisza 100-125 mandátumot nyer. Erős többség. Magyar Péter miniszterelnök lesz. Alkotmányos kétharmad azonban nincs.

Orbán alkotmányos architektúrája érintetlen marad. A Fidesz-kori Alaptörvény, a sarkalatos törvények és a feltöltött intézmények mind túlélik.

A következmények azonban túlmutatnak ezen. A következő fejezetek azt mutatják be, hogy az egyszerű többség kétharmad nélkül 60-70%-os valószínűséggel vezet a Fidesz 2030-as visszatéréséhez.

A restaurációs tézis

Ha a Tisza kétharmad nélkül nyer, a legvalószínűbb négyéves pálya: kezdeti lelkesedés, intézményi ellenállás, teljesítési kudarc, szavazói kiábrándultság, Fidesz-visszatérés. E ciklus 2030-ig 60-70%-os valószínűséggel zárul le.

Egyetlen kitörési pont létezik: ha a Tisza jogi mechanizmust talál a sarkalatos törvények felülbírálatára. A legvalószínűbb eszköz az EU-jog elsőbbségének hivatkozása (EUSZ 2. cikk), az úgynevezett „Scheppele-doktrína," amelyet a princetoni Kim Lane Scheppele javasolt. Jogilag plauzibilis, politikailag robbanékony. Még soha egyetlen kormány sem próbálta meg.

A kétharmad nélkül a Tisza legjobb reális útja az, hogy elég kompetensen kormányoz ahhoz, hogy 2030-ra a 133 mandátumra felépítse az érvet. De ugyanazok az intézményi csapdák, amelyek a reformot megakadályozzák, azokat a látható eredményeket is lehetetlenné teszik, amelyek az újraválasztást indokolnák. Ez a magyar demokratikus átmenet paradoxona: a rendszert úgy tervezték, hogy csak az a párt tudja hatékonyan működtetni, amelyik megépítette.

A 2026-os választás eldöntheti, ki kormányoz négy évig. De azt, hogy Magyarország demokratikus architektúrája ténylegesen megváltozhat-e, csak a kétharmad kérdése dönti el.
